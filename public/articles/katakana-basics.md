# Katakana Basics Guide

Katakana is one of the three writing systems used in Japanese, and it plays a distinct and indispensable role in modern written Japanese. This guide covers what katakana is, where it came from, when Japanese writers use it, and how to start reading and writing its characters.

## What is Katakana?

Katakana (カタカナ) is a phonetic syllabary — a set of characters where each symbol represents a syllable or sound rather than a meaning. It shares the same set of sounds as Hiragana, but its characters look entirely different and serve a different set of purposes.

There are 46 core katakana characters, mirroring the 46 core hiragana characters. Every sound that can be written in hiragana can also be written in katakana, and vice versa. The two syllabaries are not interchangeable in practice, however — each has well-defined contexts where it is the correct script to use.

Katakana characters are distinguished by their angular, sharp appearance. Where hiragana strokes are rounded and flowing, katakana strokes are straight lines and hard corners. This geometric quality makes katakana visually distinctive, which is part of why it is used for emphasis and foreign words.

## History and Origin

Katakana was developed during the Heian period (794–1185 CE), roughly the same era in which hiragana emerged. Both scripts derive from Chinese characters (kanji), but they took different developmental paths.

Katakana was created by Buddhist monks and scholars as a shorthand annotation system. When reading Chinese texts, scholars needed a way to add Japanese readings and grammatical notes in the margins. Rather than writing full kanji, they took abbreviated portions of existing kanji characters — often just one radical or a single stroke element — and used these as phonetic symbols. Over time these abbreviated marks stabilized into the fixed set of characters that became katakana.

The name itself reflects this origin: *kata* (片) means "partial" or "fragmentary," and *kana* refers to the phonetic scripts. Katakana was literally the "partial kana."

For most of Japanese history, katakana was used in formal and scholarly writing, while hiragana was associated with literature and personal correspondence. After the Meiji period (1868–1912), the functions of both scripts were clarified and standardized into something closer to their modern usage.

## When to Use Katakana

Understanding when Japanese uses katakana is as important as knowing the characters themselves. Katakana appears in four main contexts.

### Foreign Loanwords (外来語, Gairaigo)

This is the most common use of katakana today. When Japanese borrows words from other languages — primarily English, but also French, German, Portuguese, and others — those words are written in katakana. These borrowed words are called *gairaigo* (外来語), meaning "words from outside."

Examples:

| Katakana | Romaji | Original word |
|----------|--------|---------------|
| コーヒー | kōhī | coffee |
| テレビ | terebi | television |
| パソコン | pasokon | personal computer |
| アイスクリーム | aisu kurīmu | ice cream |
| スマートフォン | sumātofon | smartphone |
| レストラン | resutoran | restaurant |
| アパート | apāto | apartment |

Japanese phonology does not have all the sounds found in English, so loanwords are adapted to fit Japanese syllable structure. The English word "strike" becomes ストライク (*sutoraiku*), and "Christmas" becomes クリスマス (*kurisumasu*).

### Onomatopoeia

Japanese has an exceptionally rich set of onomatopoeic words — words that imitate sounds or describe sensory impressions. Many of these can be written in either hiragana or katakana, but katakana gives them a harder, more immediate quality and is commonly preferred in manga, advertising, and informal writing.

Examples:

| Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| ドキドキ | dokidoki | sound/feeling of a racing heartbeat |
| バタン | batan | a door slamming shut |
| ゴロゴロ | gorogoro | rumbling (thunder, stomach) |
| キラキラ | kirakira | sparkling, glittering |
| ザーザー | zāzā | heavy rain pouring down |

### Scientific and Technical Terms

Katakana is used to write the names of plants, animals, and biological organisms in scientific contexts. In biological and botanical literature, species names are routinely written in katakana rather than kanji or hiragana.

Examples:

| Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| サクラ | sakura | cherry blossom (botanical) |
| タンポポ | tanpopo | dandelion |
| イヌ | inu | dog (zoological) |
| ネコ | neko | cat (zoological) |

This convention helps distinguish scientific usage from everyday usage and avoids ambiguity when kanji readings might vary.

### Emphasis and Foreign Names

Katakana can be used for stylistic emphasis, similar to how English uses italics. A Japanese text might write a word in katakana to draw attention to it or to signal that it is being used in an unusual or ironic way.

Foreign personal names and place names are also written in katakana:

| Katakana | Romaji |
|----------|--------|
| アメリカ | Amerika (America) |
| フランス | Furansu (France) |
| マリア | Maria |
| ロンドン | Rondon (London) |

## Key Differences from Hiragana

Understanding how katakana differs from hiragana helps learners approach each script with the right expectations.

**Shape and stroke style.** Hiragana characters are rounded and cursive. Katakana characters are angular and constructed from short straight strokes. Compare hiragana か (*ka*) with katakana カ (*ka*) — the katakana version has two strokes and a sharp angle where the hiragana version has three strokes with curves. This difference in visual character carries through the entire script.

**Number of strokes.** Katakana characters generally have fewer strokes than their hiragana equivalents. Many katakana characters can be written in one or two strokes, making them quicker to produce once learned.

**Visual similarity traps.** Some katakana characters look similar to each other and require careful attention:
- ソ (*so*) and ン (*n*) — the angle of the two short strokes differs
- シ (*shi*) and ツ (*tsu*) — similar to the above pair, rotated
- ウ (*u*) and ワ (*wa*) — one extra stroke element distinguishes them

**Function.** Hiragana is used for grammatical elements, native Japanese words without kanji, and general writing. Katakana is used for foreign loanwords, onomatopoeia, scientific names, and emphasis. A sentence in natural Japanese will often contain all three scripts at once.

## Special Katakana Characters

Two katakana symbols appear frequently in loanwords and deserve special attention.

### The Long Vowel Mark: ー (Chōonpu)

The dash-like symbol ー is called the *chōonpu* (長音符), meaning "long sound mark." It extends the vowel of the preceding syllable by one beat. This character is used almost exclusively in katakana writing.

Examples:

| Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| コーヒー | kōhī | coffee |
| カード | kādo | card |
| ビール | bīru | beer |
| スーパー | sūpā | supermarket |

The chōonpu always follows the direction of the text. In horizontal text it is written as ー (a horizontal dash). In vertical text it rotates to a vertical bar |.

### The Small ッ (Sokuon)

The small katakana ッ (*sokuon*) indicates a double consonant — a brief pause or stop before the following consonant. It corresponds to doubled consonants in romaji transcription.

Examples:

| Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|----------|--------|---------|
| ベッド | beddo | bed |
| バッグ | baggu | bag |
| サッカー | sakkā | soccer |
| ネット | netto | net / internet |

In speech, the small ッ creates a slight hold or glottal stop before the next consonant. It takes up one beat in the rhythm of the word.

The hiragana equivalent っ works the same way in native Japanese words. The katakana ッ serves the same function specifically in katakana words and loanwords.

## Tips for Beginners

**Learn both syllabaries in parallel, not sequentially.** Many learners complete all of hiragana before touching katakana. A more efficient approach is to learn corresponding pairs together — study ア (*a*) alongside あ (*a*), then カ alongside か, and so on. Since the sounds are identical, this reinforces pronunciation while building both scripts at once.

**Group visually similar characters.** Some katakana characters share visual elements. ク (*ku*), ケ (*ke*), and コ (*ko*) all have a similar vertical stroke. Noticing these structural patterns reduces the memory load.

**Focus on loanwords you already know.** Because so many katakana words come from English, you likely already know their meanings. Reading テレビ (*terebi*) and connecting it to "television" gives you an instant memory anchor. Start with katakana versions of words you use every day.

**Practice distinguishing the confusable pairs.** The ソ/ン and シ/ツ pairs cause persistent confusion for learners. Pay attention to the direction of strokes and the angle at which the short lines are drawn. Write them side by side repeatedly until the difference is automatic.

**Use the chart as a reference, not a crutch.** Consulting the character chart during reading is fine early on, but work toward recognizing characters without looking them up. Try the [Hiragana-Katakana quiz](/hiragana-katakana-quiz) regularly to build recognition speed under mild pressure.

**Read katakana in context.** Menus at Japanese restaurants, product packaging, brand names, and sports terminology are all heavily katakana. Encountering the script in real-world contexts — even before you can read everything — trains your eye to recognize the script's shape and rhythm.

**Write by hand.** Typing katakana on a keyboard is straightforward because most input methods allow you to type romaji and convert. Writing by hand forces closer attention to stroke structure, which builds more durable memory.

## Next Steps

Once you can read and write the 46 core katakana characters and are comfortable with ー and ッ, explore the voiced variants formed with a dakuten (゛) mark — for example, カ (*ka*) becomes ガ (*ga*), and タ (*ta*) becomes ダ (*da*). Then move on to combination characters (yōon), where a small ァ, ィ, ゥ, ェ, or ォ is combined with other characters to represent sounds borrowed from foreign languages that don't exist in native Japanese phonology.

The [interactive character chart](/hiragana-katakana-chart) shows the full system including voiced and combination characters. When you are ready to test your recognition, the [Hiragana-Katakana quiz](/hiragana-katakana-quiz) lets you drill katakana in choice, dictation, and matching modes.

Katakana opens up a large portion of written Japanese immediately — menus, signs, product labels, and brand names all rely on it heavily. The 46 characters are learnable in a matter of days with focused practice, and the payoff in reading ability is immediate.
