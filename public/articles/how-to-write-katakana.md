# How to Write Katakana: Complete Guide

Katakana (片仮名) is one of the three writing systems used in modern Japanese. While it shares the same phonetic sounds as Hiragana, its characters have a distinctly angular and geometric appearance. Learning to write Katakana correctly — with proper stroke order and technique — is an essential milestone for any Japanese learner. This complete guide walks you through every character group, common pitfalls, and practical strategies for building confident handwriting.

## Introduction to Katakana Writing

Katakana is used primarily for:

- **Loanwords** from foreign languages, such as コーヒー (*koohii*, coffee) and テレビ (*terebi*, television)
- **Foreign names**, including place names and personal names
- **Onomatopoeia** and sound effects in manga and literature
- **Technical and scientific terms**, such as plant and animal names
- **Emphasis**, similar to how italics are used in English

Because Katakana is used extensively in everyday Japanese — on menus, product packaging, street signs, and advertisements — mastering it opens up a huge portion of the written language immediately. The good news is that Katakana characters tend to have fewer strokes than Hiragana, making them relatively straightforward to write once you internalize the rules.

## Basic Stroke Rules

All Japanese writing systems, including Katakana, follow a set of universal stroke order principles. Following these rules is not merely a formality — correct stroke order makes writing faster, more consistent, and more legible, especially as you progress to cursive or brush styles.

### Rule 1: Left to Right (左から右へ)

Horizontal strokes are always drawn from left to right. This applies to single strokes as well as to the overall construction of a character — the left side of a character is typically written before the right side.

**Example:** In ニ (*ni*), the top horizontal stroke is drawn left to right first, then the bottom longer stroke is drawn left to right beneath it.

### Rule 2: Top to Bottom (上から下へ)

Vertical strokes run from top to bottom, and within a character, upper components are written before lower ones. This principle keeps the pen or pencil moving in a natural downward rhythm.

**Example:** In エ (*e*), the top horizontal stroke comes first, then the vertical center stroke, then the bottom horizontal stroke.

### Rule 3: Horizontal Before Vertical (横線から縦線)

When a character contains intersecting horizontal and vertical strokes, the horizontal stroke is generally written first. After the crossbar is in place, vertical strokes pass through it.

**Example:** In ナ (*na*), the horizontal stroke is written first, followed by the diagonal stroke passing downward from it.

### Rule 4: Outer Frame Before Inner Content

For characters that have an enclosing or framing component, write the outer strokes before filling in interior details. This helps establish the overall proportion of the character before committing to internal elements.

## Writing the Vowels (ア イ ウ エ オ)

The five vowels are the foundation of Katakana. Every other character is built from one of these vowel sounds. Memorizing their stroke order first gives you a strong base.

### ア (*a*)

**Strokes: 2**

1. Draw a short diagonal stroke going down and to the left.
2. Draw a longer diagonal stroke starting from above and sweeping down to the right, crossing the first stroke.

The overall shape resembles a right-leaning chevron or an uppercase "A" without the crossbar. Keep the angle of the second stroke consistent — it should not be too flat or too vertical.

### イ (*i*)

**Strokes: 2**

1. Draw a short diagonal stroke slanting down to the right.
2. Draw a longer vertical stroke starting from slightly above and going straight down, finishing with a slight rightward flick.

The two strokes remain separate — they do not touch. The vertical second stroke should be dominant and clearly taller than the first.

### ウ (*u*)

**Strokes: 3**

1. Draw a short horizontal stroke at the top.
2. Draw a left parenthesis-shaped curve on the left side, going down.
3. Draw a matching right parenthesis-shaped curve on the right side, going down.

The two curves mirror each other, creating a rounded bottom. The small horizontal cap at the top is a single stroke and should be centered.

### エ (*e*)

**Strokes: 3**

1. Draw a horizontal stroke at the top (shorter).
2. Draw a vertical stroke from top to bottom through the center.
3. Draw a longer horizontal stroke at the bottom.

This character is highly geometric and symmetrical. Take care that the vertical stroke is truly centered between the two horizontal strokes, and that the bottom stroke is wider than the top.

### オ (*o*)

**Strokes: 3**

1. Draw a horizontal stroke at the top.
2. Draw a vertical stroke downward from the center of the first stroke.
3. Draw a curved stroke starting from the left, crossing the vertical stroke, and curving to the lower right.

The third stroke is the most complex — it begins with a leftward lean, passes through the vertical, then sweeps down and to the right. This diagonal sweep distinguishes オ from エ.

## Writing Consonant Rows

### K-Row: カ キ ク ケ コ

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| カ | ka | Horizontal stroke + rightward diagonal + downward curve |
| キ | ki | Three horizontal strokes intersected by one vertical |
| ク | ku | Short upper stroke + large rightward sweep |
| ケ | ke | Vertical + two crossing horizontals with a rightward hook |
| コ | ko | Top horizontal + short vertical on right + bottom horizontal |

**Writing カ (*ka*):** Draw a horizontal stroke first, then add a short diagonal dropping down-right from the middle, then sweep a longer curve from upper right downward to the left. The final stroke gives カ its distinctive angular personality.

**Writing ク (*ku*):** Begin with a short downward stroke in the upper right area. Then draw the dominant second stroke: start from upper left, curve across and rightward, then sweep down and to the left. The result should look like a bird's beak or a checkmark with the first stroke sitting above it.

### S-Row: サ シ ス セ ソ

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| サ | sa | Three strokes: two horizontals + one vertical with sweep |
| シ | shi | Three short strokes angled downward + one sweeping rightward |
| ス | su | Two strokes: top curve + lower cross-sweep |
| セ | se | Horizontal + vertical dropping with a base |
| ソ | so | Two short angled strokes + one longer rightward sweep |

**Writing シ (*shi*):** This character is one of the most commonly confused in Katakana. Draw two short strokes in the upper left area angling downward (like small tick marks pointing down-right). Then add a third short stroke below them in the same direction. Finally, draw the sweeping fourth stroke that goes from left upward to the right. The three tick marks are stacked nearly vertically, and the sweep goes to the right — this is crucial for distinguishing シ from ツ (see the Common Writing Mistakes section).

**Writing ソ (*so*):** Draw one short diagonal stroke in the upper left, then a second short diagonal just to its right. Both angle downward. Then add a single long sweeping stroke that starts from the upper left and arcs down to the lower right. Note that ソ's two short strokes are side by side (horizontal), while シ's are stacked (vertical).

### T-Row: タ チ ツ テ ト

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| タ | ta | Similar to ク with an added horizontal at top |
| チ | chi | Two horizontals + vertical with rightward base |
| ツ | tsu | Two short side-by-side strokes + one downward sweep |
| テ | te | Horizontal + vertical + curved base |
| ト | to | Vertical with a short rightward horizontal |

**Writing ツ (*tsu*):** Draw two short strokes side by side in the upper area, both angling downward. Then add a long stroke that curves downward from the upper left, sweeping to the lower right. The two tick-mark strokes in ツ are arranged horizontally side by side — this is the key difference from シ, where they are stacked vertically.

### N-Row: ナ ニ ヌ ネ ノ

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| ナ | na | Horizontal + vertical diagonal crossing |
| ニ | ni | Two horizontal strokes, bottom longer |
| ヌ | nu | Angled cross + downward curve |
| ネ | ne | Vertical + horizontal crossbar + two legs |
| ノ | no | Single diagonal sweep from upper left to lower right |

**Writing ノ (*no*):** This is a single-stroke character — a smooth diagonal that starts at the upper left and sweeps gracefully to the lower right. The motion is relaxed and curved, not sharp. This same sweep appears as a component inside other characters.

### H-Row: ハ ヒ フ ヘ ホ

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| ハ | ha | Two outward-angled strokes |
| ヒ | hi | Vertical with a base that kicks to the right |
| フ | fu | Single curved stroke + small horizontal at top |
| ヘ | he | Single upward-peak stroke |
| ホ | ho | Cross shape + two diagonal legs |

**Writing ハ (*ha*):** Draw two strokes that angle outward from a shared top point, like a tent or the letter "V" in a wide, open form. The left stroke is shorter and steeper; the right stroke is longer and angles more to the lower right.

### M-Row: マ ミ ム メ モ

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| マ | ma | Horizontal + downward diagonal hook |
| ミ | mi | Three horizontal strokes, each slightly longer |
| ム | mu | Large upward loop + short horizontal |
| メ | me | Cross of two diagonal strokes |
| モ | mo | Two horizontals + vertical + base horizontal |

### Y-Row: ヤ ユ ヨ

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| ヤ | ya | Diagonal + vertical with short horizontal |
| ユ | yu | Short top horizontal + vertical on right + base horizontal |
| ヨ | yo | Three horizontal strokes connected by a vertical on the right |

### R-Row: ラ リ ル レ ロ

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| ラ | ra | Short horizontal + angled drop with foot |
| リ | ri | Two vertical strokes, left shorter |
| ル | ru | Short vertical + angled sweep to lower right |
| レ | re | Single stroke angling from upper left to lower right with a foot |
| ロ | ro | Rectangular box shape |

### W-Row and N: ワ ヲ ン

| Katakana | Romaji | Key Feature |
|----------|--------|-------------|
| ワ | wa | Wide curve with a vertical on the right |
| ヲ | wo | Three strokes forming a contained shape |
| ン | n | Two short strokes + one long rightward sweep |

**Writing ン (*n*):** Draw a short downward stroke, then a second short stroke angling to the right. Finally, add a long sweep from upper left curving to the right. The sweep in ン goes to the right — this is the mirror behavior compared to ソ, and it is a frequent source of confusion for beginners.

## Common Writing Mistakes

### シ (shi) vs ツ (tsu)

This is the single most common confusion for Katakana learners. Study the table below carefully:

| Feature | シ (*shi*) | ツ (*tsu*) |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| Small strokes | Stacked **vertically** on the left | Side by side **horizontally** on top |
| Long sweep | Goes to the **right** (horizontally) | Goes **downward** (vertically) |
| Overall feel | Wider, more horizontal | Taller, more vertical |

A helpful mnemonic: **シ** looks like a face smiling to the right (the sweep is the smile going sideways). **ツ** looks like it is dripping downward (the sweep falls down).

### ソ (so) vs ン (n)

| Feature | ソ (*so*) | ン (*n*) |
|---------|----------|---------|
| Small strokes | Angled down, side by side | Angled down, slightly more separate |
| Long sweep | Curves to the **lower right** | Curves to the **upper right** |
| Overall lean | Sweeps downward and right | Sweeps upward and right |

The sweep direction is the key. ソ falls, ン rises.

### ク (ku) vs タ (ta)

Both characters share a similar upper portion, but タ has an additional horizontal stroke at the top that ク does not. When writing タ, draw the horizontal first, then proceed with the ク-like strokes beneath it.

### ロ (ro) vs 口 (the kanji for "mouth")

These two look nearly identical. Context almost always makes the distinction clear, but in terms of writing: the Katakana ロ is typically slightly wider relative to its height and tends to be written with a specific stroke order (top horizontal, right vertical, bottom horizontal joining left vertical as one stroke).

### Forgetting Dakuten (゛) and Handakuten (゜)

Voiced variants of Katakana characters are formed by adding two small marks (dakuten ゛) in the upper right corner. Semi-voiced sounds use a small circle (handakuten ゜). These marks are written as the final step after the base character is complete:

| Base | Voiced | Romaji |
|------|--------|--------|
| カ | ガ | ga |
| サ | ザ | za |
| タ | ダ | da |
| ハ | バ | ba |
| ハ | パ (*handakuten*) | pa |

## Practice Tips

### Start with Geometric Characters

Katakana's angular shapes mean that some characters are remarkably simple to draw. Begin with ー (long vowel mark), ノ (*no*), ヘ (*he*), and コ (*ko*) before tackling more complex characters. Building early confidence is important for motivation.

### Use Grid Paper

Practice on graph paper or genkouyoushi (原稿用紙, Japanese manuscript paper) with equal-sized cells. Each Katakana character should occupy one cell. Grid paper trains your eye to maintain consistent proportions — a common challenge when learning a new script.

### Write in Rows

Rather than practicing one character in isolation repeatedly, write each full row together: write カ, then キ, ク, ケ, コ, and then repeat the row. This reinforces the phonetic groupings and builds mental associations between related characters.

### Mirror Reference Examples

Keep a reference sheet visible while practicing. Write a character, then hold your own version next to the example. Look for differences in proportion, angle, and stroke length rather than just overall shape.

### Slow Down Before Speeding Up

It is tempting to write quickly to produce more output. Resist this urge early on. Slow, deliberate practice with correct stroke order will build better muscle memory than fast, sloppy repetition. Speed naturally follows accuracy.

### Practice Confusable Pairs Together

Dedicate sessions specifically to the commonly confused pairs: シ/ツ, ソ/ン, ク/タ. Write them side by side repeatedly, focusing on the specific feature that distinguishes them. Flashcard apps can help with recognition, but physical writing is essential for production.

## Writing Tools and Materials

### Pencil

A standard HB or 2B pencil is ideal for beginners. The slightly soft lead allows you to feel stroke pressure and produces a clear line without requiring a heavy hand. Pencil also lets you erase mistakes cleanly, making it the best choice while learning stroke order.

### Brush Pen (筆ペン, *fude pen*)

Brush pens are a step up from standard pencils and produce a variable line width that rewards correct technique. A soft-tip brush pen responds to pressure — pressing harder creates a thicker stroke, lifting the tip creates a finer line. Katakana's angular strokes translate well to brush technique.

### Fine-Tip Marker or Gel Pen

For everyday practice that does not require the formality of a brush, a fine-tip (0.3–0.5 mm) gel pen or rollerball produces a consistent, clean line. These are practical for notebook journaling or filling in practice worksheets.

### Japanese Grid Notebooks

Brands like KOKUYO and Campus produce grid notebooks specifically designed for kana practice. These are inexpensive and widely available online. The pre-printed cells take the guesswork out of sizing.

### Digital Tools

Tablet apps such as Kanji Study, Obenkyo, or Japanese! by Renzo allow you to practice writing on a touchscreen with real-time stroke order guidance and feedback. Digital practice is convenient for daily review, though physical handwriting practice remains valuable for developing true muscle memory.

## Next Steps

Once you can write all 46 basic Katakana characters comfortably, expand your practice to include:

- **Dakuten and handakuten variants** (ガ, ザ, ダ, バ, パ, etc.)
- **Combination characters** (キャ *kya*, シュ *shu*, チョ *cho*, etc.), where a full-size character is followed by a small ャ, ュ, or ョ
- **The long vowel mark** ー, which extends vowel sounds in Katakana words
- **Common loanwords** written in Katakana to apply your knowledge in context

## Conclusion

Writing Katakana well is a matter of understanding the underlying stroke rules, recognizing the few but important character pairs that look similar, and building consistent practice habits. The angular geometry of Katakana makes it approachable — many characters have just two or three strokes — but precision matters more here than in Hiragana, because the differences between characters are often subtle.

Focus on stroke order before speed, use grid paper to develop proportional awareness, and pay special attention to シ vs ツ and ソ vs ン until they are automatic. With steady practice, you will find that Katakana becomes a natural and satisfying script to write.
