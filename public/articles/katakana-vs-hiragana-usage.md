# When to Use Katakana vs Hiragana

Japanese has two phonetic syllabaries — hiragana and katakana — that represent the same set of sounds but serve entirely different functions. Learners often memorize both scripts and then hit a wall: given a word, which script do you actually write it in? The answer depends on the word's origin, its grammatical role, the register of the text, and sometimes the author's stylistic intent.

This guide approaches the question from a katakana-first perspective, because katakana's rules are more explicit and discrete. Once you understand where katakana is required or preferred, hiragana fills most of what remains.

---

## Overview of the Two Scripts

Katakana (カタカナ) and hiragana (ひらがな) each consist of 46 base characters, plus diacritics (dakuten ゛and handakuten ゜) and combination characters (youon). Both scripts encode the same phonetic inventory of Japanese.

The scripts differ visually: katakana characters are angular and sharp (ア、カ、テ), while hiragana characters are rounded and cursive (あ、か、て). This visual difference is not arbitrary — it maps onto a functional difference in usage that is systematic enough to follow rules, but flexible enough to leave gray areas.

If you want to see both scripts side by side with every character, the [Hiragana and Katakana Chart](/hiragana-katakana-chart) is a useful reference to keep open while working through this article.

## When Katakana Is Required

### Foreign Loanwords (外来語, Gairaigo)

This is katakana's most common function. When a word is borrowed from a foreign language and adapted into Japanese phonology, it is written in katakana. The source language is most often English, but French, German, Portuguese, and Dutch loanwords are also common.

| Source Word | Katakana | Romaji Reading |
|---|---|---|
| television | テレビ | terebi |
| apartment | アパート | apāto |
| bread (Port. pão) | パン | pan |
| part-time job (Ger. Arbeit) | アルバイト | arubaito |
| café (French) | カフェ | kafe |

Note that the katakana form may not resemble the English spelling at all — the word has been reshaped to fit Japanese phonology. Some loanwords have been in Japanese so long that learners are surprised to learn they are foreign in origin, but the katakana spelling signals this origin every time.

### Foreign Names and Place Names

People and places from outside Japan are written in katakana, regardless of how well-known they are.

- マリア (Maria)
- トーマス (Thomas)
- ニューヨーク (New York)
- パリ (Paris)
- オーストラリア (Australia)

Western company names follow the same rule: アップル (Apple), グーグル (Google), マクドナルド (McDonald's).

Japanese names, by contrast, use kanji (and sometimes hiragana for given names). Writing a Japanese person's name in katakana is unusual and typically signals something — perhaps the character is a robot, an alien, or the text is a stylistic choice.

### Scientific and Technical Names

Scientific nomenclature borrowed from Latin or European languages appears in katakana in Japanese technical writing.

- ウイルス (virus, from German *Virus*)
- バクテリア (bacteria)
- エネルギー (energy, from German *Energie*)
- ニュートン (Newton, as a unit of force)
- カルシウム (calcium)

Biological taxonomy is a particular case — see the Gray Areas section below for how animal and plant names shift between katakana and kanji depending on context.

### Onomatopoeia in Manga and Stylized Text

Japanese has an exceptionally rich inventory of sound-symbolic words. These fall into two categories: giongo (擬音語, words that mimic actual sounds) and gitaigo (擬態語, words that mimic states or textures without an actual sound). Both can be written in either script, but katakana is the dominant choice in manga, comic books, advertising, and any context where the writer wants the sound effect to feel sharp, loud, or visually striking.

| Hiragana form | Katakana form | Meaning / feel |
|---|---|---|
| どきどき | ドキドキ | heart pounding (katakana feels more intense) |
| ぴかぴか | ピカピカ | sparkling, gleaming |
| がらがら | ガラガラ | rattling, clatter |
| ふわふわ | フワフワ | fluffy, soft |

In manga specifically, sound effects like ドン (thud), ズバッ (slash), and ドカン (explosion) appear as large katakana integrated into the artwork. The script choice contributes to the percussive visual effect.

### Emphasis and Stylistic Choice

Writers sometimes use katakana for native Japanese words to make them stand out on the page — this is analogous to using italics in English.

- Neutral: がんばって (ganbatte, "do your best")
- Emphatic: ガンバッテ

This technique appears in advertising copy, manga dialogue, song lyrics, and social media. The word's meaning does not change, but it feels louder, more self-conscious, or more ironic depending on context.

In some literary fiction, katakana is used deliberately to defamiliarize a word — making a familiar concept feel foreign or clinical.

### Telegrams and Old Signage

Historically, telegrams in Japan were transmitted in katakana because it was cheaper and more efficient to encode. Old public signage, especially from the Meiji and Taisho eras, also used katakana for formal or official text. This usage is largely archaic, but you will encounter it in historical photographs, period films, and museum exhibits.

## When Hiragana Is Required

### Native Japanese Words (Yamato Kotoba)

Words with deep Japanese origins — the vocabulary that predates the large-scale borrowing of Chinese and later Western words — are written in hiragana when no kanji form is used or known.

- ありがとう (arigatou, thank you)
- たくさん (takusan, many / a lot)
- ところ (tokoro, place)
- やはり (yahari, as expected / indeed)

Many of these words have kanji spellings that exist but are rarely used in modern writing. The hiragana form is the default.

### Particles

Grammatical particles are always written in hiragana. There are no exceptions.

- は (wa) — topic marker
- が (ga) — subject marker
- を (wo) — object marker
- に (ni) — directional / locative
- へ (e) — directional
- で (de) — location of action / means
- と (to) — and / with / quotation
- か (ka) — question marker
- も (mo) — also / even
- の (no) — possessive / nominalizer

Writing particles in katakana would be a serious error. Even in stylized or creative text, particles remain in hiragana.

### Verb and Adjective Conjugations (Okurigana)

The inflectional endings attached to verb and adjective stems — called okurigana — are written in hiragana. The stem may be written in kanji, but the ending is always hiragana.

- 食べる (taberu, to eat) — る is hiragana
- 食べました (tabemashita, ate) — ました is hiragana
- 高い (takai, expensive / tall) — い is hiragana
- 高くない (takakunai, not expensive) — くない is hiragana
- 走っている (hashitte iru, is running) — っている is hiragana

The polite endings ます and です, and the copula だ, are always hiragana.

### Children's Writing and Furigana

Children's books and educational materials for early learners are often written entirely in hiragana, with no kanji, because children learn hiragana before kanji. School textbooks introduce kanji gradually, adding hiragana as furigana (小さい文字) above kanji to show the reading.

As a learner, writing in hiragana when you are unsure of the correct kanji is acceptable and common. Hiragana is the fallback; katakana is not.

## Gray Areas and Style Choices

### Company Names and Brand Names

Japanese companies have considerable latitude in how they render their own names. Many major companies use katakana for their Japanese-market name even if the company name is of Japanese origin, because katakana reads as modern, international, and corporate.

- ソニー (Sony) — Japanese company, katakana
- トヨタ (Toyota) — Japanese company, katakana
- キヤノン (Canon) — Japanese company, katakana

But some companies use kanji or a mix:
- 任天堂 (Nintendo) — kanji in formal use, though ニンテンドー appears in some contexts
- 東芝 (Toshiba) — kanji

Foreign companies almost always appear in katakana when written in Japanese characters.

### Animal and Plant Names: Scientific vs Casual

In biological and scientific contexts, animal and plant names are often written in katakana to signal the technical register, even when the same animal has a perfectly normal kanji name in everyday writing.

| Casual (kanji/hiragana) | Scientific/formal (katakana) |
|---|---|
| 猫 or ねこ (cat) | ネコ |
| 犬 or いぬ (dog) | イヌ |
| 桜 or さくら (cherry blossom) | サクラ |
| 蛸 or たこ (octopus) | タコ |

In a biology textbook or field guide, you will see ネコ. In a novel or casual sentence, you will see 猫 or ねこ. Neither is wrong; the choice reflects context and register.

### The Emphasis Technique: Use It Sparingly

Using katakana to emphasize a native Japanese word is a recognized technique, but overuse makes text feel chaotic or ironic when irony is not intended. In formal writing — business documents, academic papers, news articles — this technique is avoided entirely. In informal, creative, or youth-oriented text, it appears regularly.

## Common Mistakes by Learners

**Writing foreign loanwords in hiragana.** Some learners, having mastered hiragana first, default to it for everything. Writing こーひー instead of コーヒー for "coffee" is incorrect in standard Japanese.

**Writing native Japanese words in katakana without intent.** If you write たべる as タベル, a native speaker will interpret this as deliberate stylistic emphasis or as a writing error. It will read as strange.

**Forgetting particles and verb endings.** Even when the main verb stem is written in kanji, the endings must be hiragana. The sentence 食べマシタ is incorrect; the correct form is 食べました.

**Applying English logic to foreign words.** Not every foreign word in Japanese is written in katakana exactly as it sounds in English. The katakana represents how the word was borrowed into Japanese phonology, which may look very different from the original. アイスクリーム (ice cream) is the correct form; not アイス・クリーム with a separator in this case, though the dot separator ・ is used in some compound loanwords like ニューヨーク・タイムズ.

**Assuming katakana names are always foreign.** Some Japanese given names are written in katakana — this is a legitimate stylistic choice, particularly for names registered in katakana. Do not assume that every katakana name is non-Japanese.

## Quick Reference Decision Chart

Use this flowchart when you are unsure which script to use:

1. Is the word a grammatical particle or verb/adjective ending?
   - Yes → **Hiragana**

2. Is the word borrowed from a foreign language (gairaigo)?
   - Yes → **Katakana**

3. Is the word a foreign person's name or a non-Japanese place name?
   - Yes → **Katakana**

4. Is the word a scientific or technical term borrowed from a European language?
   - Yes → **Katakana**

5. Is the word a sound effect or onomatopoeia in a manga or stylized text?
   - Yes → **Katakana** (preferred)

6. Is the word a native Japanese word with no applicable kanji?
   - Yes → **Hiragana**

7. Is the writer using katakana for deliberate emphasis on a native word?
   - Yes → **Katakana** (intentional, context-dependent)

8. Default:
   - Native Japanese word with a kanji form → **Kanji** (with hiragana okurigana as needed)
   - Native Japanese word without a kanji form → **Hiragana**

## Practice: Identify the Correct Script

For each word below, decide whether it should be written in katakana, hiragana, or kanji with hiragana. Answers follow.

1. "smartphone" (from English)
2. The particle meaning "from" (から)
3. "cherry blossom" in a biology textbook
4. "thank you" (ありがとう)
5. "Berlin" (the city)
6. The verb ending of 食べる (eat) in past tense
7. "energy" in a physics textbook
8. "dog" in a casual sentence

**Answers:**

1. スマートフォン — katakana (foreign loanword)
2. から — hiragana (particle, always hiragana)
3. サクラ — katakana (scientific/biological register)
4. ありがとう — hiragana (native Japanese expression)
5. ベルリン — katakana (foreign place name)
6. 食べました — the stem 食べ uses kanji, the ending ました is hiragana
7. エネルギー — katakana (borrowed technical term)
8. 犬 or いぬ — kanji (犬) is standard; hiragana (いぬ) is acceptable; タヌキ would be incorrect here

The most effective way to internalize these patterns is exposure to real Japanese text — menus, packaging, subtitles, news articles — where you can observe the choices writers make. To test your recognition of individual characters in both scripts, the [Hiragana and Katakana Quiz](/hiragana-katakana-quiz) offers practice across all character types.

Script choice in Japanese is rule-governed but not mechanical. The rules above cover the vast majority of cases. The gray areas and stylistic uses come with reading experience, and native speakers themselves sometimes make judgment calls. What matters most is understanding why katakana exists and what it signals — foreignness, technical precision, visual emphasis — because those signals are present every time you read or write Japanese.
