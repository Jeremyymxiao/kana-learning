# Common Katakana Words Guide

Katakana is used in Japanese primarily to write foreign loanwords — a category that covers an enormous slice of everyday vocabulary. Thousands of words from English, French, German, Portuguese, and other languages have been borrowed into Japanese and adapted to fit its sound system. Written in katakana, these words appear on menus, product labels, signs, screens, and in conversation constantly. Learning to read them gives you immediate access to a large and practical vocabulary without needing to learn new meanings from scratch — you often already know what the words mean.

This guide covers common katakana loanwords organized by topic. Each word is shown with its katakana spelling, romaji pronunciation, English meaning, and the language it was borrowed from.

If you want to review the katakana character set before working through these words, the [Hiragana-Katakana chart](/hiragana-katakana-chart) shows every character, including voiced variants and combination sounds. Once you are ready to test your reading, the [Hiragana-Katakana quiz](/hiragana-katakana-quiz) lets you drill recognition across all character types.

## Why Learn Katakana Words

Katakana loanwords reward learners at every level. For beginners, they offer an early payoff: once you can read the 46 katakana characters, many words are immediately readable because you already know their meanings. For intermediate learners, they fill vocabulary gaps quickly and expose you to how Japanese phonology adapts foreign sounds.

There are a few things worth knowing before working through the word lists:

**Japanese phonology changes borrowed sounds.** Japanese syllables follow a consonant-vowel pattern and lack many consonant clusters found in English. "Strike" becomes ストライク (*sutoraiku*), with vowels inserted to separate the consonants. "Bed" becomes ベッド (*beddo*), with the final vowel added. Some sounds have no direct equivalent — English "v" becomes バ (*ba*) or ビ (*bi*), and English "th" is rendered as ス (*su*) or ズ (*zu*). The romaji column in each table below shows the Japanese pronunciation.

**Long vowels are marked with ー.** The dash-like symbol ー (*chōonpu*) extends the preceding vowel by one beat. コーヒー (*kōhī*) represents the two long vowels in the English word "coffee."

**Loanwords are abbreviated.** Japanese frequently shortens borrowed words. "Personal computer" becomes パソコン (*pasokon*), "air conditioner" becomes エアコン (*eakon*), and "convenience store" becomes コンビニ (*konbini*). These shortened forms are the standard forms used in everyday speech.

## Practice Section

The word lists above cover a broad range of everyday situations. The following exercises will help you move from recognizing these words on the page to recalling them quickly.

### Reading Practice

Work through each table and cover the romaji column. Read each katakana word aloud and check your pronunciation against the romaji. Pay attention to the long vowel mark ー and the double consonant mark ッ, both of which are easy to miss when reading quickly.

Pay particular attention to words that look similar:

- コーヒー and コイン — note the ー after each コ and the different vowel sounds that follow
- テレビ and テーブル — テ is the same but the words diverge immediately after
- バス (bus) and パス (pass) — バ and パ differ only by the dakuten (゛) mark on バ

### Writing Practice

Choose ten words from any category and write each one from memory five times. Writing reinforces character shapes more durably than reading alone. Then test yourself by writing the katakana for an English word — for example, write "hotel" in katakana before checking the table.

### Recognition Drills

Use the [Hiragana-Katakana quiz](/hiragana-katakana-quiz) set to katakana mode to build character-level recognition speed. Fast character recognition is the foundation for reading full words without hesitation. Once character recognition feels automatic, return to reading the word tables in this guide at a faster pace.

### Building Vocabulary in Context

The most effective way to internalize katakana vocabulary is to encounter it in real contexts. Reading Japanese menus, looking at product packaging, or reading the katakana on a Japanese website or game interface exposes you to these words in situations where meaning is clear from context. When you see テレビ written on an electronics store sign or コーヒー on a cafe menu, the connection between script and meaning becomes immediate and durable.
