# Essential Hiragana Phrases for Beginners

Learning hiragana is one of the first milestones in your Japanese journey, but knowing the characters is only the beginning. The real payoff comes when you can read and use actual Japanese phrases written in hiragana. This article walks you through the most practical everyday phrases, all written in hiragana so you can practice reading them while also picking up real conversational vocabulary.

Before diving in, if you need a refresher on individual characters, check out the [Hiragana & Katakana Chart](/hiragana-katakana-chart) to review any characters you are uncertain about.

---

## Basic Greetings

Greetings are the gateway to any conversation. Japanese has time-specific greetings for morning, afternoon, and evening, and knowing them immediately makes you sound natural and polite. Many of these phrases appear in their full hiragana form in everyday writing and texting.

| Hiragana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| おはようございます | ohayou gozaimasu | Good morning (formal) |
| おはよう | ohayou | Good morning (casual) |
| こんにちは | konnichiwa | Hello / Good afternoon |
| こんばんは | konbanwa | Good evening |
| おやすみなさい | oyasumi nasai | Good night (formal) |
| おやすみ | oyasumi | Good night (casual) |
| さようなら | sayounara | Goodbye (formal, final) |
| じゃあね | jaa ne | See you / Bye (casual) |
| またね | mata ne | See you later |
| またあした | mata ashita | See you tomorrow |

A note on こんにちは: the final character は is the topic particle, pronounced "wa" rather than "ha" in this fixed phrase. This is one of the quirks of written Japanese that beginners often encounter early.

## Common Expressions

These are the phrases that come up constantly in daily life. Native speakers use them so frequently that they become almost automatic. Mastering these gives you a solid foundation for real conversations.

| Hiragana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| はい | hai | Yes |
| いいえ | iie | No |
| ありがとうございます | arigatou gozaimasu | Thank you (formal) |
| ありがとう | arigatou | Thank you (casual) |
| どういたしまして | dou itashimashite | You're welcome |
| すみません | sumimasen | Excuse me / I'm sorry |
| ごめんなさい | gomen nasai | I'm sorry (apology) |
| ごめん | gomen | Sorry (casual) |
| だいじょうぶです | daijoubu desu | It's okay / I'm fine |
| だいじょうぶ | daijoubu | It's okay (casual) |
| わかりました | wakarimashita | I understood |
| わかりません | wakarimasen | I don't understand |
| もういちどおねがいします | mou ichido onegaishimasu | Please say that again |

The distinction between すみません and ごめんなさい is worth noting. すみません is used to get someone's attention, to apologize for minor inconveniences, or to express gratitude when you feel you are imposing on someone. ごめんなさい is a direct, sincere apology for something you did wrong.

## Essential Daily Phrases

These phrases appear in situations you encounter every single day: meals, moving around, entering and leaving places, and basic needs. Memorizing these will help you feel more comfortable from the moment you wake up.

| Hiragana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| いただきます | itadakimasu | Let's eat (said before meals) |
| ごちそうさまでした | gochisousama deshita | Thank you for the meal (said after eating) |
| ただいま | tadaima | I'm home |
| おかえり | okaeri | Welcome back |
| いってきます | ittekimasu | I'm leaving (said when going out) |
| いってらっしゃい | itterasshai | Take care / Have a good day |
| おねがいします | onegaishimasu | Please (requesting something) |
| どうぞ | douzo | Please (offering something) / Go ahead |
| はじめまして | hajimemashite | Nice to meet you |
| よろしくおねがいします | yoroshiku onegaishimasu | Nice to meet you / Please treat me well |

いただきます and ごちそうさまでした are deeply embedded in Japanese culture. Saying them before and after a meal is considered good manners regardless of the setting, whether at home, in a restaurant, or eating at your desk at work.

はじめまして and よろしくおねがいします are almost always used together when meeting someone for the first time. The phrase よろしくおねがいします has no perfect English equivalent — it roughly conveys the hope that you will get along well and that the other person will be patient with you.

## Polite Phrases

Japanese has multiple levels of politeness built into the language itself. As a beginner, using polite forms (teineigo) is always the safe choice when speaking with people you do not know well. These phrases all fall into the polite register and are suitable for most everyday situations.

| Hiragana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ～をください | ~ wo kudasai | Please give me ~ |
| ～はどこですか | ~ wa doko desu ka | Where is ~? |
| ～はいくらですか | ~ wa ikura desu ka | How much is ~? |
| みてもいいですか | mite mo ii desu ka | May I look at this? |
| てつだってもらえますか | tetsudatte moraemasu ka | Could you help me? |
| えいごははなせますか | eigo wa hanasemasu ka | Can you speak English? |
| にほんごがすこしはなせます | nihongo ga sukoshi hanasemasu | I can speak a little Japanese |
| ゆっくりはなしてください | yukkuri hanashite kudasai | Please speak slowly |
| かいてください | kaite kudasai | Please write it down |
| もすこしまってください | mou sukoshi matte kudasai | Please wait a little longer |

The pattern ～をください is one of the most immediately useful constructions you will learn. Slot any noun before をください and you have a polite request: みずをください (mizu wo kudasai) means "please give me water," and メニューをください (menyuu wo kudasai) means "please give me the menu."

## Question Phrases

Questions form the backbone of getting information, and a handful of key question words unlock a wide range of conversations. These words — who, what, when, where, why, how — are known as the "5W1H" in Japanese learning circles.

| Hiragana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| なに / なん | nani / nan | What |
| どこ | doko | Where |
| だれ | dare | Who |
| いつ | itsu | When |
| なぜ / どうして | naze / doushite | Why |
| どう | dou | How |
| どれ | dore | Which one |
| いくら | ikura | How much (price) |
| いくつ | ikutsu | How many |
| なんじですか | nanji desu ka | What time is it? |
| おてあらいはどこですか | otearai wa doko desu ka | Where is the bathroom? |
| これはなんですか | kore wa nan desu ka | What is this? |
| いまなんじですか | ima nanji desu ka | What time is it now? |
| ここはどこですか | koko wa doko desu ka | Where is this place? |

These question words attach naturally to the polite sentence-ending pattern ～ですか (desu ka), which is the standard way to turn a statement into a yes/no question, or combined with the question words above to form information-seeking questions.

## Putting It Together: Short Conversations

Reading individual phrases in a table is useful, but seeing them in context helps solidify how they work together. Here are two short sample exchanges written entirely in hiragana.

**Meeting someone new:**

> あ：はじめまして。たなかです。よろしくおねがいします。
> *(A: Hajimemashite. Tanaka desu. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.)*
> *(A: Nice to meet you. I'm Tanaka. I look forward to knowing you.)*

> び：はじめまして。すみます。よろしくおねがいします。
> *(B: Hajimemashite. Shimizu desu. Yoroshiku onegaishimasu.)*
> *(B: Nice to meet you. I'm Shimizu. Likewise.)*

**At a restaurant:**

> きゃく：すみません。みずをください。
> *(Kyaku: Sumimasen. Mizu wo kudasai.)*
> *(Customer: Excuse me. Water, please.)*

> てんいん：はい、どうぞ。
> *(Ten'in: Hai, douzo.)*
> *(Staff: Yes, here you go.)*

## Practice Exercises

Reading about phrases is not enough — you need to actively practice them. Here are some exercises to solidify what you have learned.

**Exercise 1: Matching**
Cover the romaji and English columns and try to read each hiragana phrase aloud. Check your pronunciation against the romaji afterward. Focus especially on phrases where は is used as a particle and pronounced "wa."

**Exercise 2: Time-of-day greetings**
For one full day, practice using the correct greeting at the correct time: おはようございます in the morning, こんにちは in the afternoon, and こんばんは in the evening. You can say them quietly to yourself or write them down.

**Exercise 3: Meal phrases**
Every time you eat or drink something, practice saying いただきます before and ごちそうさまでした after. This builds the habit so it feels natural when you are actually in Japan or speaking with Japanese speakers.

**Exercise 4: Question construction**
Take the question words from the Question Phrases section and practice combining them with ですか. Try forming simple questions like これはなんですか or おてあらいはどこですか until they come naturally.

**Exercise 5: Take a quiz**
Once you feel confident reading these phrases in hiragana, test your overall hiragana reading ability with the [Hiragana & Katakana Quiz](/hiragana-katakana-quiz). Recognizing individual characters quickly makes reading full phrases much smoother.

## Final Thoughts

The phrases in this article cover a wide range of everyday situations — greetings, meals, requests, apologies, and questions. The key to making them stick is consistent, low-pressure repetition. Do not try to memorize everything at once. Start with the greetings and common expressions, then gradually add the daily phrases and polite forms as they feel comfortable.

Reading these phrases in hiragana rather than romaji from the start is worth the extra effort. When you see こんにちは and immediately recognize it as a greeting rather than having to decode it character by character, that is when the script starts to feel natural. Keep practicing, and that moment comes sooner than you expect.
