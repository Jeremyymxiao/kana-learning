# Japanese Loanwords in Katakana

One of the first things you notice when studying Japanese is the abundance of words borrowed from other languages — especially English. These borrowed words, written in katakana, appear everywhere: on menus, signs, packaging, and in everyday conversation. Once you recognise the patterns behind how foreign words are adapted, reading katakana becomes far easier and more intuitive.

This article covers the full picture of Japanese loanwords: where they came from, how they were adapted phonetically, common examples from several languages, and a few tricky cases that catch learners off guard.

---

## What Are Loanwords (Gairaigo 外来語)?

The Japanese term for a loanword is **gairaigo** (外来語), literally "words that came from outside." These are words borrowed from foreign languages and incorporated into Japanese vocabulary. They are almost always written in **katakana**, which is one of the primary functions of that script.

Gairaigo are distinct from two other categories of borrowed language:

| Category | Kanji | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Gairaigo | 外来語 | Words from non-Chinese foreign languages | テレビ (television) |
| Kango | 漢語 | Words borrowed or derived from Chinese | 電話 (denwa, telephone) |
| Wasei-kango | 和製漢語 | Japanese-coined words using Chinese characters | 経済 (keizai, economy) |

Gairaigo make up a substantial portion of modern Japanese vocabulary. Estimates suggest that roughly 10% of everyday Japanese words are loanwords, rising to much higher proportions in fields like technology, fashion, food, and sports.

See the [Hiragana-Katakana Chart](/hiragana-katakana-chart) for a complete reference to the katakana syllable set used to write these words.

## History of Loanwords in Japanese

Japanese has been borrowing foreign words for centuries. The language's history of contact with outside cultures maps directly onto the origin of its loanword layers.

### Portuguese and Spanish (16th Century)

The arrival of Portuguese missionaries and traders in the 1540s introduced the first wave of European loanwords. Many of these words relate to food, religion, and trade goods that were new to Japan.

| Portuguese | Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| pão | パン | pan | bread |
| tabaco | タバコ | tabako | tobacco |
| capa | カッパ | kappa | raincoat |
| carta | カルタ | karuta | playing cards |
| tempero | テンプラ | tenpura | tempura (frying method) |

### Dutch (17th–19th Century)

During Japan's period of national isolation (sakoku), the Dutch were the only Europeans permitted to trade through Nagasaki. This gave Dutch an outsized influence on Japanese scientific and technical vocabulary.

| Dutch | Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| bier | ビール | bīru | beer |
| glas | ガラス | garasu | glass |
| pompe | ポンプ | ponpu | pump |
| ransel | ランドセル | randoseru | school backpack |
| gas | ガス | gasu | gas |

### English (20th Century to Present)

After Japan's Meiji Restoration (1868) and especially following World War II, English became the dominant source of loanwords. The massive influx of American culture, technology, and commerce from the mid-20th century onwards means that English-origin gairaigo now far outnumber those from any other source language.

## How Loanwords Are Adapted

Japanese phonology differs significantly from English and other European languages. When a foreign word enters Japanese, it must be mapped onto Japanese syllable structure, which consists almost entirely of consonant-vowel (CV) pairs. This adaptation follows predictable rules.

### Key Sound Changes

| Rule | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| L → R | Japanese has no L sound; L is replaced with R | light → ライト (raito) |
| TH → S or Z | No TH sound in Japanese | three → スリー (surī) |
| V → B | No V sound in native Japanese | violin → バイオリン (baiorin) |
| Final consonants get vowels added | Japanese syllables rarely end in a consonant | desk → デスク (desuku), bed → ベッド (beddo) |
| Long vowels are marked with ー | Vowel length is preserved | cake → ケーキ (kēki) |
| F → F or H | F exists in modern katakana but historically became H | fork → フォーク (fōku) |
| W → W before A, elsewhere dropped | ウ row handles most W sounds | word → ワード (wādo) |
| Double consonants become ッ | Geminate stop is inserted | bed → ベッド (beddo) |

### Added Vowels at Word Endings

Because Japanese syllables must end in a vowel (except ン), consonant clusters at the end of foreign words receive a supporting vowel. The default added vowel is **u**, except after T or D where it is often **o**.

| English | Katakana | Romaji |
|---|---|---|
| milk | ミルク | miruku |
| disk | ディスク | disuku |
| coat | コート | kōto |
| test | テスト | tesuto |
| staff | スタッフ | sutaffu |

## Loanwords from Other Languages

While English dominates modern gairaigo, several other languages have left clear traces in Japanese vocabulary.

### Portuguese

| Portuguese | Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| pão | パン | pan | bread |
| tabaco | タバコ | tabako | tobacco |
| capa | カッパ | kappa | raincoat / river imp (homophone) |
| botão | ボタン | botan | button |
| jarra | ジャーロ | — | jar (less common today) |

パン (pan, bread) is so deeply embedded in Japanese that many speakers do not recognise it as a loanword at all. It appears in compound words like 食パン (shokupan, sandwich bread) and パン屋 (panya, bakery).

### Dutch

| Dutch | Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| bier | ビール | bīru | beer |
| glas | ガラス | garasu | glass |
| ransel | ランドセル | randoseru | school backpack |
| pompe | ポンプ | ponpu | pump |
| orgel | オルゴール | orugōru | music box |

ランドセル (randoseru) is a particularly interesting case — the distinctive hard-shell backpack carried by Japanese schoolchildren is named after the Dutch word for military pack, a term that entered Japanese during the 19th century.

### French

| French | Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| croissant | クロワッサン | kurowassan | croissant |
| café au lait | カフェオレ | kafē ore | café au lait |
| atelier | アトリエ | atorie | artist's studio |
| bouquet | ブーケ | būke | flower bouquet |
| debut | デビュー | debyū | debut |

French loanwords tend to cluster around fashion, cuisine, and the arts — domains where French cultural influence has been strong globally.

### German

| German | Katakana | Romaji | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| Arbeit | アルバイト | arubaito | part-time job |
| Energie | エネルギー | enerugī | energy |
| Allergie | アレルギー | arerugī | allergy |
| Thema | テーマ | tēma | theme / topic |
| Gips | ギプス | gipusu | plaster cast |

アルバイト (arubaito) — often shortened to バイト (baito) — deserves special mention. The German word means "work" in general, but in Japanese it has narrowed to mean specifically part-time or temporary work, particularly the kind done by students.

## "Wasei-eigo" — Japanese-Made English Words

**Wasei-eigo** (和製英語, "Japanese-made English") refers to words that look and sound like English loanwords but were actually coined in Japan. They are not used in English and will not be understood by native English speakers.

| Wasei-eigo | Katakana | Romaji | What Japanese speakers mean | Actual English equivalent |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| salary man | サラリーマン | sarariiman | a white-collar office worker | office worker, salaryman |
| consent | コンセント | konsento | electrical outlet / socket | outlet, socket |
| claim | クレーム | kurēmu | to complain to a company | complaint |
| cunning | カンニング | kanningu | cheating on an exam | cheating |
| mansion | マンション | manshon | a concrete apartment building | apartment, condo |
| handle | ハンドル | handoru | a steering wheel | steering wheel |
| freeter | フリーター | furītā | a person doing only part-time work | gig worker, freelancer |
| skinship | スキンシップ | sukinshippu | physical affection / closeness | physical bonding |
| viking | バイキング | baikingu | an all-you-can-eat buffet | buffet |
| paper driver | ペーパードライバー | pēpā doraibā | a person with a licence who never drives | — |

These words cause genuine confusion for both Japanese speakers visiting English-speaking countries and for English speakers learning Japanese. コンセント (konsento) is one of the most commonly cited traps — a learner who asks for a "consent" in an English-speaking country will not find an electrical outlet.

## False Friends and Tricky Loanwords

Beyond wasei-eigo, several loanwords have shifted in meaning or are pronounced so differently that they are hard to recognise.

| Katakana | Romaji | Apparent English word | Actual meaning in Japanese |
|---|---|---|---|
| マンション | manshon | mansion | A modern reinforced-concrete apartment building — nothing grand |
| テンション | tenshon | tension | Excitement, high energy, good mood (not stress) |
| スマート | sumāto | smart | Slim / slender (not intelligent) |
| ナイーブ | naību | naive | Sensitive / fragile-hearted (not foolish) |
| サービス | sābisu | service | Something given for free, a freebie |
| ペット | petto | pet | Companion animal only (not a pet name / favourite) |
| カーブ | kābu | curb | A curve in the road (not the edge of a pavement) |
| シーズン | shīzun | season | Also used to mean seasoning / spice |

The shift in **テンション** is particularly important for learners. Saying someone has "high tension" (テンションが高い) in Japanese is a compliment meaning they are lively and excited — the opposite of the English association with stress or anxiety.

## Putting It Together

Gairaigo are a gateway into Japanese. Because many loanwords from English are phonetically close to their originals, a learner who masters katakana can immediately read and make educated guesses at a large slice of Japanese vocabulary on signs, menus, and packaging.

The key steps are:

1. **Learn katakana fluently.** You cannot decode loanwords until you can read the script automatically. Use the [Hiragana-Katakana Chart](/hiragana-katakana-chart) as a reference and the [Kana Quiz](/hiragana-katakana-quiz) to drill recognition until it is effortless.
2. **Memorise the sound change rules.** L → R, V → B, added vowels, long vowel marks — these patterns cover the vast majority of adaptations you will encounter.
3. **Watch for false friends.** Wasei-eigo and shifted meanings are the main source of confusion. When a loanword does not seem to mean what you expect, check whether its Japanese meaning has diverged.
4. **Notice abbreviations.** Japanese loanwords are frequently truncated: テレビ from television, スーパー from supermarket, スマホ from smartphone. The shortened forms are what speakers actually use.

With these principles in mind, the katakana on any Japanese menu, shop window, or product label becomes far less opaque — and often quite familiar.
